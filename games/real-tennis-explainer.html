How Real Tennis Works

A Quick Guide for Lawn Tennis Players

Real tennis is the original racket sport, played since the 1500s in enclosed stone courts across Europe. The scoring is familiar - 15, 30, 40, game - but the court, the walls, and the chase system make it a completely different game.

Games and Sets

Games

First to 6 games wins the set. Unlike lawn tennis, you don't need to win by two - a set can finish 6-5. No tiebreaks.

Sets

Matches are typically best of 3 sets (social play) or best of 5 (competitive). Advantage sets are played - no tiebreakers.

Deuce

At 40-40 in level play, advantage rules apply (like lawn tennis). In handicap matches, 40-40 is sudden death - next point wins.

Handicaps

Every player has a handicap number. The difference between players sets the starting score each game - weaker players get point head-starts.

Serving

Hit the Roof

Serving in real tennis is nothing like lawn tennis. The ball must hit the sloped penthouse roof and land in the service box.

  1. The server stands on the service side (left of the net in the diagram above)
  2. The ball must hit the penthouse (the sloped roof along the gallery wall) on the hazard side
  3. It must then bounce into the service box on the hazard side floor
  4. The receiver returns from the hazard side
  5. Two serves per point (like lawn tennis) - unless handicap rules say otherwise
Key principle: Length over power. A deep, awkward bounce is almost always better than a fast, shallow one. "Even a bad railroad is a 25-handicap serve."

The Serve Motion

Unlike lawn tennis, the serve is an underarm stroke. The ball is scooped upward to hit the sloped penthouse roof, then drops into the service box on the other side of the net.

Side view: the ball arcs from the server's hand (1), hits the penthouse (2), slides along the slope (3), and drops into the service box (4)
1

Ready

Stand behind the service line. Ball in non-racket hand, racket held upright.

2

Drop & Load

Bend forward, drop the ball in front. Racket swings low behind as the body coils.

3

The Scoop

Sweep the racket forward and up under the ball. The underarm motion imparts backspin and loft.

4

Follow-through

Racket arm finishes high. Watch the ball arc toward the penthouse. Weight on front foot.

The 9 Serve Types

Each serve has a different trajectory, spin, and tactical purpose. Most players rely on 2-3 and mix in others to disrupt rhythm.

1 Railroad Serve Workhorse

▾
Technique
Change grip (forehand face as mirror). Racket back over shoulder. Toss about 2 feet above your head. Step forward left. Strike the underside with an across-and-under motion. Follow through past your left leg.
What it does
Ball travels down the edge of the penthouse with speed and spin, bounces on the floor, and spins back towards the Winning Gallery. One of the hardest serves to return.
When to use
Your go-to first serve. Reliable, effective at every level. Aim for one bounce on the penthouse, one on the floor, hitting the back wall low and tight.

2 Bobble Serve Safe option

▾
Technique
Strike without spin, just above knee height. Aim for it to land halfway up the penthouse above Hazard First Gallery. The ball "bumbles" up in an arc, nearly touching the top of the penthouse, then drops near the grille wall.
What it does
Forces awkward footwork with a late, low drop. The unpredictable bounce makes it hard to time a clean return.
When to use
Good first serve or safer option. Play it shorter than you think.

3 Boomerang Serve Chaos

▾
Technique
Similar to the Bobble but firmer. Aim to land the ball onto the service penthouse beyond the Winning Gallery. The ball arcs toward the grille penthouse, hits the high back wall, and returns across the side penthouse.
What it does
Exploits the full geometry of the court. Creates complexity and hesitation.
When to use
Sparingly, against players who struggle with quick lateral movement. High unpredictability.

4 Sidewall Serve Spin

▾
Technique
Face the main wall, racket above shoulder with elbow bent. Step left and hit the underside with an open face, generating cut and sidespin. Ball hits the service wall, spins down to the penthouse, bounces about 6 feet from the grille wall.
What it does
Slow bounce with awkward spin that pulls the ball back towards the tambour. Pulls the receiver out of position.
When to use
Effective against receivers who volley early. Good when you have a strong chase to protect.

5 Underarm Twist Spin

▾
Technique
Start with racket high and to the side. Drop the ball in front, swing down and across with a cut motion applying sidespin. Generates a low, spinning bounce.
What it does
The heavy spin makes the ball hard to control on the return. Stays low after bouncing.
When to use
Against players who struggle with spin or depth. Focus on spin and placement, not power.

6 Demi-Pique Tactical

▾
Technique
Hit into the penthouse above Hazard First Gallery with sidewall action. Ball bounces up towards the service wall, grazing the penthouse on the way down.
What it does
Creates indecision. The receiver can't tell whether to volley or let it bounce.
When to use
Ideal against volleyers. Precision over pace - the uncertainty is the weapon.

7 Drag Serve Finesse

▾
Technique
Uses sidewall action. Ball curves through the air and lands right on the edge of the penthouse above Hazard First Gallery, then dies in the receiver's corner.
What it does
Tight angles catch the receiver flat-footed. Forces a late, low contact.
When to use
When aiming for precision. Requires great control - think finesse, not force.

8 Drop Serve Surprise

▾
Technique
Hit from near the main wall using sidewall motion, just below waist height. Aim toward the rafters. If it hits the penthouse, it plops into the back corner.
What it does
Sudden change in pace and bounce height. Breaks the receiver's rhythm.
When to use
As a surprise. High risk for second serves - use sparingly.

9 Giraffe Serve Height

▾
Technique
Underarm twist action, strike above knee height aiming high toward the beams. Ball bounces quickly off the penthouse, runs tight along the back wall.
What it does
Pushes the receiver deep and tight against the back wall. Forces weak returns.
When to use
To exploit opponents who are weak off the back wall. Emphasise height as much as spin.
Rallies & Walls

Everything Is In Play

In lawn tennis, the ball is out if it hits a wall. In real tennis, walls and roofs are your weapons.

  • ✓
    Main wall - The solid wall opposite the galleries. Hit the ball off it, play it off the rebound. All fair game.
  • ✓
    Penthouse roof - The sloped lean-to above the galleries. Balls can bounce off it, roll along it, and drop back into play.
  • ✓
    Back walls - Both end walls are in play. A ball hitting the back wall and coming back into court is still live.
  • ✓
    Tambour - The angled buttress at the hazard end of the main wall, right next to the grille. Balls hitting the tambour deflect at unpredictable angles, creating some of the most dramatic moments in the game.
  • ✓
    Volley or one bounce - Same rule as lawn tennis: you can volley or play after one bounce. If the ball bounces twice, it sets a chase (more on that next).

Three Winning Targets

Hit the ball into any of these and you win the point outright - no questions asked:

◆

Dedans

Large netted opening at the back of the service side. The most dramatic winning shot - like a goal in football.

■

Grille

Small netted opening low on the hazard end back wall. A precise, satisfying target - hard to hit but impossible to defend.

▶

Winning Gallery

The gallery opening nearest the net on the hazard side. Hit the ball into it from anywhere on court to win the point.

The tambour in action: A ball hitting the angled tambour wall can shoot off at wild angles. Good players use it as a weapon; everyone else fears it. Three scenarios: ball hits the tambour directly (run backwards, then step in), ball narrowly misses it (one step, high boast), or short balls near it (early step, aggressive strike).
The Chase

The Unique Twist

This is what makes real tennis unlike any other racket sport. In lawn tennis, two bounces ends the point. In real tennis, it creates a deferred contest.

Step 1 of 5

The Ball Bounces Twice

During a rally, a player hits a great shot and their opponent can't reach it. The ball bounces once, then bounces again on the floor.

In lawn tennis, the point would be over. In real tennis, the spot where the ball bounced the second time is noted - this is now a "chase".

Step 2 of 5

The Chase Is Marked

The marker (a real person at the side of the court) calls out the position of the second bounce using the chase lines on the floor. These lines run across the court at regular intervals.

For example: "Chase better than 4" or "Chase the door" (if the ball entered a gallery).

Closer to the back wall = a "better" chase = harder for your opponent to beat.

Step 3 of 5

Play Continues

The chase is "stored" but the game continues as normal. Points keep being played.

Players change ends when:

  • Two chases have been laid, OR
  • One chase is laid and the score reaches 40 (i.e. "chase and 40")

The end change is when the chase gets resolved.

Step 4 of 5

Players Change Ends

When the end change happens, the player who set the chase now moves to the side where the chase was marked. Their opponent must try to "beat the chase".

To beat it, they need to make their shot's second bounce land closer to the back wall than the chase mark - or hit a winning target (dedans, grille, or winning gallery).

Step 5 of 5

Why It Matters

The chase system rewards good length. A ball that dies near the back wall sets a chase that's almost impossible to beat. A weak shot that bounces twice near the middle of the court sets an easy chase.

Think of it like setting a high jump bar: you're daring your opponent to clear it. The better your shot, the higher the bar.

Strategic depth: At 40-0, take the easy point - no need to risk giving away a chase. At deuce, chase management becomes critical. As Rob Fahey says: "Play the odds."

Court Features Glossary

Penthouse

Sloped lean-to roof running along the gallery wall. Serves must hit it. Balls roll along it and drop off the edge.

Dedans

Large netted opening at the service end back wall. Hit the ball into it to win the point outright.

Grille

Small netted opening on the hazard end back wall. Another instant-win target - precise and satisfying.

Tambour

Angled buttress at the hazard end of the main wall, next to the grille. Creates unpredictable deflections.

Winning Gallery

Gallery opening nearest the net on the hazard side. The third instant-win target.

Galleries

Openings along the gallery wall beneath the penthouse. Balls entering galleries can set chases or win points.

Service Box

Marked area on the hazard side floor where the serve must bounce after hitting the penthouse.

Chase Lines

Lines across the floor marking positions. Used to record where a ball bounced twice (setting a chase).

Hazard Side

The side of the court to the right of the net (in our diagram). The receiver stands here. Contains the grille.

Service Side

The side to the left of the net. The server stands here. Contains the dedans.

Main Wall

The solid wall opposite the galleries. No openings. Balls played off it are in play.

Marker

The official who calls the score, records chases, and manages the game. Sits in the dedans.

Key Differences from Lawn Tennis

  • Open court, ball goes out vs Enclosed court, walls are in play
  • Two bounces = point over vs Two bounces can set a chase
  • Serve anywhere in the box vs Serve must hit the penthouse roof first
  • Symmetrical court vs Asymmetric with galleries, tambour, grille
  • Tiebreaks at 6-6 vs No tiebreaks. Can win a set 6-5
  • Universal handicap rare vs Every player has a handicap number
  • Leader's score called first vs Server's score always called first
