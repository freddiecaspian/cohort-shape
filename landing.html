The Origin

I built a transcription tool to process my INSEAD lecture recordings. Single audio file, one microphone, zero pre-configuration. I pointed it at a live class and hit run.

Without being told anything about the people in the room, the system was inferring speaker nationalities from accents, tracking conversational dynamics across sessions, identifying who was leading discussion and who was invisible - not in a single class, but longitudinally, over months.

Then the system did something I didn't expect. It found my co-founder.

Across 165 transcripts, the AI ranked Chris Johnson as the #1 contributor in our 90-person section. Professors' own feedback confirmed it independently. 65 minutes after I showed Chris the analysis, we'd mapped the product, the privacy architecture, the go-to-market, and shaken hands.

The product that will assess classroom participation is the same product that identified its own co-founder. That's not a pitch. That's proof.

The Problem

High-stakes assessment,
low-fidelity tools

Participation counts for 30-50% of the grade at top business schools. Assessment centres test the most human qualities. And the observation infrastructure for both? A notebook.

Higher Education

One professor. 90 students. 15 sessions. Memory.

A PhD researcher sits in every OB session with a notebook, tracking 90 students across a term. Professors grade participation weeks later from recall. Recency bias. Personality bias. No longitudinal view. The assessment is simultaneously high-stakes and low-fidelity.

30-50% of the final grade at top MBA programmes - assessed from memory
Assessment Centres

Two assessors. Six candidates. No replay.

While you're writing notes on Candidate A, Candidates B and C make points you miss entirely. The exercise happens once. If a borderline decision comes down to "did she build on his point or just repeat it?" - nobody can check. Assessor quality at 9am vs 4pm? Different.

100s of assessment centres per hiring cycle at a single firm - assessor fatigue is structural

Why Audio

the transcript cannot

- Marwan Sinaceur, INSEAD Professor of Organisational Behaviour
  • 01

    Privacy by design

    A single microphone is categorically less invasive than cameras on every face. Consent is simpler. Audio is processed, insights delivered, raw recording deleted.

  • 02

    Scalability

    One device. Cheap to capture, store, and process. Video multiplies cost and complexity by an order of magnitude.

  • 03

    Sufficiency

    For participation assessment, voice carries the signal that matters: who spoke, how they spoke, what they said, who responded. We're not adding surveillance. We're upgrading assessment.

The big vision - once privacy architecture and efficacy are proven in simpler contexts first.

Participation counts for just 10% of the grade at most schools. Not because it doesn't matter - because it's too painful to assess properly.

Right now, grading participation means a professor manually tracking 90 students across 15 sessions, cross-referencing handwritten notes with fading memory, spending hours per student on subjective recall. Some schools hire a dedicated PhD researcher just to sit in the room and observe. The assessment burden is so high that schools suppress the weighting rather than do it properly.

Remove that burden and the weighting goes to 30-40% - where professors always believed it should be. That's a Jevons Paradox: make assessment cheaper and easier, and schools do more of it, not less. The product doesn't just serve the existing market - it grows it.
